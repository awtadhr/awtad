Facing the Truth

صُمٌّ بُكْمٌ عُمْيٌ فَهُمْ لَا يَرْجِعُونَ · أَوْ كَصَيِّبٍ مِّنَ السَّمَاءِ فِيهِ ظُلُمَاتٌ وَرَعْدٌ وَبَرْقٌ يَجْعَلُونَ أَصَابِعَهُمْ فِي آذَانِهِم مِّنَ الصَّوَاعِقِ حَذَرَ الْمَوْتِ ۚ وَاللَّهُ مُحِيطٌ بِالْكَافِرِينَ

Summun bukmun 'umyun fahum laa yarji'oon. Aw kasayyibin minas-samaa'i feehi dhulumaatun wa ra'dun wa barq, yaj'aloona asaabi'ahum fee aadhaanihim minas-sawaa'iqi hadhara al-mawt, wallaahu muheetun bil-kaafireen.

Deaf, dumb, and blind — so they do not return [to guidance]. Or [their case is] like a rainstorm from the sky carrying darkness, thunder, and lightning: they press their fingers into their ears against the thunderclaps, dreading death. But Allah surrounds the disbelievers entirely.

Surah Al-Baqarah — Verses 18–19

Some truth unsettles the soul before it benefits it, and some advice frightens before it guides. This page helps you check yourself: do you face the truth and benefit from it, or do you cover your ears the way some cover theirs against the thunder?

Summary of the meaning

These two parables describe someone turning away from guidance. The first is like a person who lit a fire, only for Allah to take away its light, leaving them in darkness unable to see. The second is like someone caught in a storm of darkness, thunder, and lightning, who plugs their ears against the thunderclaps out of fear of death. The lesson: truth can sometimes shake the soul and expose its flaws, and some people run from hearing it rather than benefiting from it — even though Allah is fully aware of them and nothing about their situation escapes Him. Running from the truth changes nothing about reality.

A short summary drawn from classical commentary, in plain language — not a direct quotation from any single source.
